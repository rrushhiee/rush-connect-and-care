Mentoring approach

My approach to youth mentoring

Updated 28 April 2026

Finding the right youth mentor for a young person can be a big decision. As a parent, carer, or support coordinator, you may be looking for a steady male youth mentor who can build trust, provide guidance, encourage positive choices, and help a young person develop practical life skills in a way that feels natural and respectful.

My youth mentoring approach is relationship-based, practical, and goal-focused. I work with young people through consistent one-to-one support that helps them build confidence, emotional regulation, independence, communication skills, and stronger community connection.

Every young person is different, so I tailor sessions around their interests, personality, goals, and support needs. The aim is for mentoring to feel relaxed and genuine, while still having purpose and direction.

Relationship first

Trust comes before advice. Many young people take time to open up, especially if they feel judged, pressured, or misunderstood.

  • a calm and respectful space
  • support without lecturing
  • consistency, patience, and follow-through

Letting the young person lead

I listen first. Mentoring works best when the young person feels heard and involved, not managed.

  • what they enjoy and care about
  • what they find hard
  • what motivates them
  • what they want their future to look like
  • what support feels helpful

Practical support that builds real skills

Mentoring should help in real life, not stay stuck in talk. The focus is on small wins that build confidence and momentum.

  • conversation and reflection
  • community activities, fitness, and social outings
  • goal planning and problem-solving
  • building routine, confidence, and independence

Practical questions

How sessions often open up conversation

During sessions, I may ask questions that help the young person reflect on their week, recognise progress, and think about what they can do differently next time.

  • How has your week been?
  • What activities have you done?
  • What has gone well recently?
  • What has been difficult?
  • What skills have you been working on?
  • What is something you would like to do better?
  • What is one small thing we can work on today?

Goals and direction

Goal setting and direction

Mentoring is most effective when it has direction. I support young people to think about what they want for their future and then break that down into small, achievable steps.

Some goals may be big, such as getting a job, becoming more independent, improving relationships, or building confidence. Other goals may be smaller, such as getting out of the house more, joining an activity, or improving communication.

The goal is not to pressure the young person. The goal is to help them see what is possible and support them to take one step at a time.

Helpful prompts

  • What would your dream job be?
  • What kind of life would you like to build?
  • What are you interested in?
  • What would you like to get better at?
  • What is one step you could take towards that goal?
  • What support do you need to get there?

For parents and carers

Why this may help your young person

Space outside the home

A youth mentor can be helpful when a young person needs a consistent, positive adult in their life who is not a parent, teacher, or therapist.

Some young people find it easier to open up to someone outside their immediate family. Mentoring can give them space to talk, reflect, practise skills, and build confidence without feeling judged or overwhelmed.

Common reasons families enquire

  • struggling with confidence
  • finding it hard to communicate
  • feeling isolated
  • lacking motivation
  • needing support with routine
  • having difficulty managing emotions

Longer-term growth

  • unsure about the future
  • needing help to build independence
  • needing positive connection outside the home
  • working towards NDIS goals

Mentoring is not about fixing a young person. It is about walking alongside them, helping them understand themselves, and supporting them to build practical skills for everyday life.

Safe and respectful support

A safe, respectful, and goal-focused approach

I understand the importance of safe boundaries, clear communication, and respectful support. Sessions are designed to be relaxed and engaging, but they are also linked back to meaningful goals. Mentoring does not replace crisis, emergency, or clinical support, and I work within clear boundaries about what mentoring can and cannot offer.

Where appropriate, I can also communicate with parents, carers, or support coordinators about general progress, goals being worked on, and areas where the young person may need further support.

Mentoring philosophy

Build trust. Listen first. Keep it practical.

Set goals. Celebrate small wins.

I believe young people are more likely to grow when they feel respected, understood, and supported by someone who is consistent and genuine.

Through youth mentoring, I aim to help young people feel more confident, more capable, and more connected to the people, places, and opportunities around them.
